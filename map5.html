Rogue-Umpqua Scenic Byway

Distance: 172 miles, Allow: 5 to 7 hours

The Rogue and Umpqua Rivers are two of Oregon's most storied, both for their world famous fishing and rafting and for their primal beauty. Traditionally a timber town, Roseburg today is known for recreational fishing, and a growing number of wineries and microbreweries. From Roseburg, the byway leads east through scenic oak-dotted hills and meets up with the North Umpqua near Glide. Traveling upstream, the byway follows the North Umpqua where you're likely to spot anglers casting for trout, salmon, and summer steelhead, native oceangoing rainbow trout that can reach 20 pounds. Hikers and mountain bikers are captivated by the North Umpqua Trail that winds for 79 miles along the river's banks. With more than 15 cascades along its route, the byway has earned the nickname "Highway of Waterfalls." Well-marked trails provide several overlooking views of the river's canyons.

Past Toketee Lake, OR-138 bits farwell to the North Umpqua and turns south to skirt the shores of Diamond Lake. Framed by 9,200-foot Mt. Thielsen and 8,363-foot Mt. Bailey, Diamond Lake is a jewel of the Cascade Mountains. At the end of Diamond Lake OR-138 continues south toward Crater Lake National Park. The Rogue-Umpqua Scenic Byway joins OR-230 and swings southwest into the Rogue River-Siskiyou National Forest, where the Rogue emerges from underground lava tubes. The byway joins OR-62 just north of Union Creek, the western terminus of a wagon road for settlers crossing the Cascades. The byway passes several campground and fishing spots as it follows the Rogue downstream. At Joseph H. Stewart State Recreation Area, the river widens into Lost Creek Lake. Eventually the byway joins OR-234 and ends at Gold Hill, which is less than an hour from Medford, Ashland, and Grants Pass.
